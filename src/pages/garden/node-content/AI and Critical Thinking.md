> [[PenPen]]'s Note: This is a letter which was later published to the blog, so some context may be lost. Also, this topic is hard and there are no "right" answers. What follows isn't intended to be construed as fact, just a perspective formed after much thought.

On May 7th, *New York Magazine* published an article proclaiming that [“Rampant AI Cheating is Ruining Education Fast.”](https://nymag.com/intelligencer/article/openai-chatgpt-ai-cheating-education-college-students-school.html) In it, teachers lament about students' apparent inability to think for themselves. Troy Jollimore, a Cal State ethics professor, states that “Massive numbers of students are going to emerge from universities while being essentially illiterate, both in the literal sense and in the sense of having no knowledge of their own culture, much less anyone else’s.” Lakshya Jain, a lecturer at UC Berkeley, warns that by intertwining themselves with the robot, students have made it trivial for the robot to replace them outright.

They, as the teachers can attest, are not wrong.

I am ashamed to announce that critical thought is dead, both in and out of the classroom.

But, who is really to blame?

I mean, clearly, students would rather be elsewhere, hanging with their friends or engulfed by their magic box of algorithmically injected dopamine.

But, I mean, they’re going to college? They’re taking AP classes? Clearly, they must want to learn? How can that possibly be so, if they are truly perverse to thought?

Critical thinking is dead, but it isn’t ChatGPT who killed it, nor the students who use it. It’s been dead for a long time.

In aggregate, school doesn’t reward thought. It makes no effort to foster it. The students who succeed are not the ones who think the most—it’s the ones who give the most of themselves to the machine. Students must go through all of the motions, memorize all of the facts, and write their 50th analytical essay about a book that doesn’t speak on their behalf. If you ask ChatGPT what to write about, it will provide the most average thought. If you follow its instruction to a T, you walk away with an A. In school, it is better to be ‘safe’ than to take a chance on a risky idea that might fail.[^1]

With that being said, is it really any surprise that ChatGPT is this popular? I can’t teach others if I don’t know for myself. AI cannot help in a discourse between peers. A program confined to the walls of data centres in Virginia cannot have any impact on a local community. But students aren’t knowledge workers. They work in a factory, incessantly producing endless & identical artifacts that are discarded at the end of the year as readily as an impulse buy from the TikTok shop. In the real world, you don’t get criticized for easing your job, so long as your productive output remains identical. Hell, that is the entire foundation of the industrial revolution. In school, students use AI to write the most stunningly mundane essays of their lives. The end is the same, but the means have changed. Students are giving teachers exactly what they asked for, and they are getting better and better at doing it.

Several candidates for school committee voiced their intent to give my town one of the best schools in the country. So,

- Instead of running from controversial issues, or relegating discussions to a few school sanctioned activities each year, find ways to incorporate them in classes. In History, for instance, find connections between historical events and those unfolding today.
- Abandon the current push to eliminate screens in schools. Find ways to incorporate them further. Teach students how to code & use Desmos. Encourage students to reason about and experiment with how AI could be reasonably used, given its strenths and weaknesses.
- Incorporate originality of thought into grading criteria. Dramatically increase the amount of in-class discussions. In English, spend more time encouraging students to introspect by writing about lived experiences and finding connections with stories. Give students opportunities to demonstrate knowledge in their own way. Give greater weight to project-based learning. Guide students to ask why. Encourage iterative work with a strong feedback loop.
- Immediately instantiate a mandatory course that covers critical thinking, media literacy, and good-faith debate of contentious topics. Teach students how to disagree, and how to see things from a different perspective. Equip them with the tools to handle current issues head-on and recognize disinformation.

What I propose is really damn hard. I am asking you to reorganize your curriculum around things that humans do best—things that we *must* do best if we are to retain our humanity. It will cost money. It will require training. It requires rethinking what a school should be, and its relationship with the students. Undoubtably, it will be an uphill battle. But to be great you must be bold.

Just as the Industrial Revolution ushered in a new era of modern convenience, AI has demonstrated its excellence in eliminating the busy work that now consumes our daily lives. Positioning the school as a bastion for the free thought that is so uniquely human will make it emerge as a leader in a new frontier, a world of unparalleled advancement. Journalists at ProPublica [use AI](https://www.propublica.org/article/using-ai-responsibly-for-reporting) to comb through thousands of documents, looking for critical moments to use in their exposés. DeepMind’s AlphaFold [predicts](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-021-03819-2) protein structures with near-experimental level accuracy. Neuralink[^2] uses AI to give paralysed folks the blessing of mobility. Society is accelerating, and your students shouldn’t get left behind.

All this is new and scary. But I must remind you that AI has not once had an original thought, it must be trained on what we know. So to the students—to all those wishing to make real change—I urge you to not sit idly while schools meander aimlessly, still committed to the way things used to be. You must take charge, and devote your life to becoming the very best at what you do. Every moment of your life should be a quest for knowledge. Learn more about yourself, your communities, your family, and your friends. Learn more about your job, your hobbies, and the natural world. Expose yourself to new ideas, new people, and new experiences. By becoming a well-rounded individual with the gift of thought, you poise yourself to become a leader wherever you go—a leader that some robot cannot replace. Yes, be a good scholar, but take risks, make mistakes, be bold, have fun, and seize every damn opportunity you’re blessed with.

God speed. Love, Evan.
