If, a couple of weeks ago, you were to ask me to make a list of perfect things, my Garmin watch would be on it.

In a world of bright and beautiful screens, subscriptions, and the attention economy, Garmin presented something different. Their watches featured a dull screen, convoluted UI, and underpowered hardware. In exchange, you were rewarded with an exceptional battery, the most advanced fitness tracking on the market, and... peace.

The watch is perfectly happy to chug along without Bluetooth. In fact, their high-end watches ship with offline maps of your continent, and plenty of space for music, should you wish to go off the grid entirely. If you *do* decide to connect to the internet, Garmin offers a web platform — Garmin Connect. The promise appeared to be that the Garmin would retain high quality activity data in their cloud for as long as you'd like.[^1] If wanted to *delete* this data, that's also cool — Garmin received wide praise for its [privacy policy](https://foundation.mozilla.org/en/privacynotincluded/garmin-fenix/).

How was it possible for Garmin to create unshittified devices profitably? Well, you pay a pretty penny. High end Garmin watches, such as the Fenix 8, fetch upwards of $1000, and Garmin is infamous for only updating watch software for a year or so after release. The price, however, is more than worth it. I've had my watch for 6 years, and it chugs along happily without complaint. Zero planned obsolescence, just exactly what I paid for. What a breath of fresh air.

I had assumed that Garmin possessed an astute understanding of their relationship with their users. The simplicity of the model set them apart. Keep providing us with well-crafted products, and we will buy them for whatever price you set, under the expectation that the product will last peacefully until we are ready for an upgrade. This is why we keep coming back. This is why we pay $1000 for a watch, when even the Apple Watch Ultra costs less.

I had assumed that Garmin, having masterfully carved out its own little niche that protected it from its competitively priced smartwatch competitors, would get this. 

I was wrong.

Money always corrupts.

Today, Garmin had the audacity to announce its subscription service — Garmin Connect Plus. As far as I can tell, no features were stripped from the Connect platform, and as such I am not yet angry. I'm still getting exactly what I paid for. I don't expect new features for a watch I bought 6 years ago, be that on-device or in-app. This play is fair enough.

What I am, however, is concerned. For the most part, the new subscription-only features pander mostly to a technophile demographic, not Luddites like me.[^2] Amongst the features are an AI trainer, educational videos, exclusive challenges, and more advanced social features. I don't care about any of this. I am not the target audience. I just want my data. I want the confidence that it will always be there. I want the confidence that when I want it, I will get only what I want, instead of being assaulted by advertising for something I don't need.

If Garmin had said "we're not coming for your data," "you're not our target audience," or literally any other assurance to quell my fears, I'd be inclined to believe them. I've never witnessed them go against their word, and they've built a lot of trust over these years.[^3] But they haven't said anything of this sort, and today I learned that I cannot put words in their mouth. Implicit promises about our relationship are useless. From now on, I must assume that unless they *say* they won't do something, they very likely will.

So, here's where this is going:

First, the subscription features will feel fair and unobtrusive. They will add more social features. Expand their video library.

An upgrade button will appear in the corner.

Then, new data features will appear under the subscription. This is already happening, a little bit. The new performance dashboard feature doesn't add *new* data, but provides a different view of it. It is locked behind a paywall.

![[Pasted image 20250327212237.png]]

In this regard, what they are currently selling is new ways to *process* your data, with advanced dashboards and AI insight. But the data is still yours. New visualisation features will target the subscription. Existing visualisation features will wallow.

And as they wallow, more and more upsells will appear, telling you all the fun you're missing out on while using legacy features. Sometime around this point, subscription features will begin popping up on the watch.

And then, one by one, the existing features will be deprecated. In 2012, Garmin [added the ability](https://www.dcrainmaker.com/2012/05/look-at-tonights-hard-brick-workout.html) to view live statistics from in-progress activities on your phone. It sounds convenient. Say you're in the middle of a long run and want to plan out a route based on your previous track. Virtually all other competitor platforms offer it. But it died along with the Garmin Fit app. Today, it is reintroduced, for only $6.99 a month.[^4] This will happen more.

And finally, the inevitable, the culmination of all the shit shoved down our throats. The motherload, so to speak. We will lose our data. Maybe, this will come in the form of some covert ToS change, as Mozilla did to Firefox just one month ago. Maybe, new retention policies will be announced, because *who could want activity data from 5 years past? if you want it, you'll want a subscription! Only $6.99!!* Maybe, one day, they will dare to gate a hardware feature behind a subscription. It is hard to predict when or where, but it will come. It always does, even if [27 years down the line](https://www.mozilla.org/en-US/about/history/).

![[Pasted image 20250327214122.png]]

I expect Garmin to affirm that this will not be the case. I have reached out to them, asking four simple questions. If they provide a satisfactory response, I will be disproven. If they maintain silence—the most likely outcome—that silence will speak volumes.

![[Pasted image 20250327220426.png]]

It's funny. Just a couple of weeks ago, it had occurred to me that it is perhaps time for an upgrade, as a little birthday gift to myself. The watch still works just as it did on day one, but I almost felt it fair to do so. A [[Old Tech|perfect device]] is so rare in today's world, and I wish to support those who produce them.

Thank god I didn't hit purchase. I'll need to wait it out for at least a couple of years, see where it goes. There's no hard feelings — I've got so much value in these past 6 years. But I'll think twice before purchasing another watch.

As for alternatives, it's looking not too great. Suunto and Coros (a Chinese company) offer competing adventure devices, however to the best of my knowledge neither have promised to not follow suit.

I may buy a rePebble just out of principle — it runs the open source and community developed PebbleOS. The rePebble, however, is not a sufficient replacement. While it does feature a heart rate monitor, step counter, and 30 day battery. I'm excited about a watch I can hack. But it lacks the advanced fitness features Garmin has cultivated over years. It doesn't even have GPS. It's a different watch, for a different person.

So for now we wait and pray for sunshine.
