### [Beep Beep](https://daringfireball.net/2023/12/beep_beep)
[Tapestry: What About?](https://furbo.org/2024/03/03/tapestry-what-about/)

I thought that beeper would be a philanthropic endeavor. Insisted, I would say.

Once, beeper said this:

> We—of course—expected a response. What we didn’t expect was 1984-esque doublespeak. The statement is complete FUD. Beeper Mini made communication between Android and iPhone users more secure. That is a fact.

I then said this:

I have no doubts beeper will win this battle. The only way apple wins is a lawsuit. Every other piece of communication is inherently reproducible – at the end of the day it's a computer running code sending radiowaves, and if you can reproduce those waves, you're in. There is no way to make a service only certain devices can use, though you can make it hard. Unfortunately, making communication more difficult breaks backwards compatibility, something Apple is reluctant to do.

It feels clear Beeper won't give up. Eric Migicovsky is pretty rich and seems pretty pissed. He can afford to burn money and also according to beeper, they were the fastest growing paid android app of all time, so there is clearly profit to be made. The only ways I see this ending are:

1. Apple gives up. Beeper continues to exist
2. Apple sues beeper. Apple wins. (By what law?)
3. Apple sues beeper. Beeper wins. Walled garden takes a gigantic hit.
4. One of the above, cat leaves the bag, Apple is forced to have their own answer to beeper

One possibility is iMessage Android, but it requires an iCloud subscription.

1. they can extract wealth from android users
2. they can protect the blue bubble
3. they can undercut beeper's price
4. no risk to garden

Regardless, save for outcome 2, beeper has done a good for the world. It's a sound business model for a man worth roughly 30 million dollars.

Plus, seeing David (the high schooler) fight Goliath is pretty cool.

I was wrong.

Regardless, I need to butt heads with John again. They don't provide unlimited full resolution attachments – the files count against iCloud storage limits. The moderation is nonexistent. If Beeper wants to pay the cost of supplying the service to Android users, let them. The Centurion Lounge analogy is deeply flawed as:

1. There are no social pressures associated with obtaining membership in Centurion Lounge
2. Centurion Lounge does not deliberately diminish the experience of airport users outside of said lounge (deliberately implementing bad compression algos)
3. As Centurion Lounge has a finite amount of physical space, the presence of non members diminishes the experience of members. In fact, where the presence of non-members *would* benefit members, it is allowed (the guest pass). The presence of iMessage non-members certainly benefits the majority of members.

Still, we've been blessed with an interesting question disguised as a statement:

> Being really good at competing is not anticompetitive.

I'd like to dwell on this. In due time.

### Have your cake and eat it (CoHost is Dying)

[CoHost – March 2024 Financial Update](https://cohost.org/staff/post/5023717-march-2024-financial)

CoHost has just announced they have 3 weeks to find more profit. They report a net income of -$12k. It would be much lower, but their plushies sold well. The problem is, their investor is AWOL and doesn't want to give more money anyway. This sudden news comes after giving themselves all a raise from $87k to $94k.

The problem here is that CoHost wants to have their cake and eat it too. You see, they are particularly picky about how they will monetize CoHost. They don't have venture capital. Still, this group of friends wants to pay themselves regular tech salaries. I really hope they can figure out *how* to do this, but assuming it was even possible was the mistake. Starting a company is *hard*. I feel like they think it's supposed to be just as comfortable as a regular job. No. It's much harder. It's riskier, it requires more time. Like all investments, you should only put in what you can afford to lose. When you're self bootstrapping, you should be minimizing expenses to the greatest possible extent. You should not be shocked when your steady draw of 30k a month from the bank eventually empties it. Certainly, the mindset "if we can just launch this new app now we'll surely be profitable" isn't helpful. Look. If you want a company done right, you need to suffer a little bit.

All I'm saying is this naive "we'll figure it out later" strategy can work if you don't feel a responsibility to users, so you get some VC money and just sell out before you even figure out how to turn a profit. Fair play. Like genuinely. If you *don't* plan on doing this, playing it by ear is a cringe move.
#### Based and Fedi-Pilled

On the Fediverse, instances are, by design, run by volunteers. I've heard analogies made between D&D masters and Fedi admins, and I agree with this. At the small scale of these servers, it's possible for it to be done relatively economically for an instance admin, both in terms of cost and time devoted to moderation. The strength is that profitability doesn't need to happen. The [enshittification](https://pluralistic.net/2023/01/21/potemkin-ai/#hey-guys) cycle – literally *born* from companies needing to figure out how to be profitable – simply won't happen here. Mastodon will forever be a place by the community for the community. The primary con is volatility. Volunteers, especially as they are not profit driven, may pack up their bags and leave their instance to rot at any time. Thankfully, Mastodon is pretty good at preserving data, if you know where to poke around.

### iCloud is winning me over

Right now, I pay \$12 for iCloud's 50gb storage tier, which I have run out of. I also pay \$20 a year for Migadu's email service. iCloud happens to include email for custom domains on the paid tiers. You could combine these into iCloud's \$3/m tier for only 4 additional dollars, while receiving an additional 150gb of cloud storage. Pretty solid deal.

The question is (well, after the "is iCloud mail enough" one), is this the right move? I want to continue to support Migadu, but the price increase is... A lot... And as discussed above, some business models are... Poor. I imagine not many consumers are interested in the "we provide the same service as big-tech for 3x the price, but we are not big tech so it's fine" pitch.

But then again, Migadu is awfully cute. Well, I have another 9 months to decide.

### [A message to our amazing Affinity community](https://affinity.serif.com/en-us/press/newsroom/canva-statement/)

*[Discussion on the Fediverse](https://social.coop/@eb/112162108859905127)*

I write a lot of open source code. At one point in my life, I would have categorized myself as a FOSS absolutist. Some of the best programs are open. The thing about Affinity is yeah, sure there is Gimp and Inkscape. Sure, they have 90% of the features, and yet, the OSS community is missing designers (which is ironic as these are design tools). Features don’t matter if it takes 10x longer to use them because of endless submenus.  
Another thing about the open source community: leadership is immensely difficult. Very few are strong-willed enough to reject code that probably shouldn’t be accepted. Even fewer possess the power to boss developers around, which can easily happen at a company. It’s unsurprising nobody wants to work on the hard edges that keep cutting users. Nobody is forcing them to.  

[Darktable: crashing into the wall in slow-motion](https://ansel.photos/en/news/darktable-dans-le-mur-au-ralenti/)

> I just remembered a specific example where they have (had? It's been years) sliders for things that were a _single pixel tall_. To select them, even if you were perfectly sighted and otherwise abled, was almost impossible. I should be color correcting my photos, not trying to get the mouse to click on the single pixel contact point. But all the reports were just closed with "it works well enough for us and saves space, click better."

That's hilarious, and a perfect example of how sometimes programmers really do need a few designers in their life. In regards to proprietary, it's certainly a careful calculus for me. Like, at the peak of my FOSS absolutism, I still used Steam, and then I asked myself "why" and I realized that Steam was one of the only pieces of software where I wasn't constantly thinking "I could do x better myself". Affinity is another one of those.

What I mean to say is I hope Gimp can eventually replace Affinity, but I’m not holding my breath. They’ve had a long time.
