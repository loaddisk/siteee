When you look up 'Weird Massachusetts Laws,' one of the first results is from Morgan & Morgan. Hidden away in their FAQ is a great deal of such 'laws,' without any links for verifiability:

![[Pasted image 20250402181237.png]]

Archive.org snapshots first show the page appeared in May 2023. Blogs from [Miller Law Group](https://www.apmillerlawgroup.com/blog/2023/june/7-quirky-massachusetts-family-laws-still-on-the-/) and [Beacon Hill](https://bhsg.com/resources/strange-business-laws-in-massachusetts) parade similar claims. News agencies are also in on the fun—[Boston.com](https://www.boston.com/news/local-news/2015/06/18/weird-laws-in-massachusetts/) features a malformed listicle published in June 2015, and [Fox News](https://www.foxnews.com/us/weird-laws-massachusetts) coverage from Ashlyn Messier wins the Boehs prize of journalistic integrity for being the only publication to reference the actual laws.

With some of these laws feeling insane, I wanted to do a little fact checking, so here we go!

**"At a wake, mourners are not allowed to eat any more than three sandwiches"**

To the best of my understanding, this is false. [`239 CMR 3.00, 3.11 (3)`](https://www.mass.gov/doc/239-cmr-3-registration-requirements-standards-of-business-and-professional-practice/download) places restrictions on the folks serving the food, but not on what the mourners may eat. I found a mention of it in this [archived](https://web.archive.org/web/20120112170300/http://huntnewsnu.com/2011/11/mass-laws-raise-eyebrows/) 2011 article, however rather puzzlingly, the link now redirects to this image:

![[Pasted image 20250402183513.png]]

My best guess of the origin of this myth is [former prohibitions on all eating at funerals](https://www.bostonmagazine.com/news/2013/04/30/funeral-home-food-ban-massachusetts/). Deceased senator Thomas Kennedy was, apparently, hell-bent on getting this law to change for over a decade, before it finally did right around his own passing.

**Snoring is also prohibited—unless every bedroom window is securely locked and closed**

This law has been repeated verbatim by lawyers in [other states](https://www.facebook.com/JayGoinsLaw/posts/do-you-know-these-louisiana-laws1-you-may-not-tie-an-alligator-to-a-fire-hydrant/1025590082904180/). Neither 'snoring' nor 'bedroom' is mentioned in this context at malegislature.gov. `310 CMR 7.10, 7.10` describes the rules regarding noise pollution and doesn't make any references to sleeping folk. The closest thing I could find was [`272, § 53`](https://malegislature.gov/laws/generallaws/partiv/titlei/chapter272/section53), which states that "keepers of noisy and disorderly houses ... shall be punished." This is a common-law offence which is now used almost exclusively to shut down house parties.[^2]

**Damaging a milk container or carton is punishable by a $10 fine**

This is true.

> Whoever, without the consent of the owner thereof, knowingly and wilfully effaces, alters or covers over, or procures to be effaced, altered or covered over, the name, initial or device of any dealer in milk, marked or stamped upon a milk can, or whoever, with intent to defraud and without such consent, detains or uses in his business any such can having the name, initial or device of any dealer in milk so marked or stamped thereon, shall be punished by a fine of not more than ten dollars.

Fox attributes this to `255 § 128`, however I found it in [`266 § 128`](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartIV/TitleI/Chapter266/Section128). Morgan & Morgan advises that this law is obsolete because new milk comes in jugs, not in paper cartons, however the real law makes no mention of paper cartons.

**It is illegal to take a bath unless you've been instructed to do so by a physician**

Boston.com also writes that "In Boston, it is illegal to bathe unless your doctor gives you a prescription. BUT, it’s also illegal not to bathe before going to bed in Boston." I've seen other variations floating around, like one stating that those who bathe more than twice a month are subject to severe punishment. Is any of this true? Well, no, not as far as I can see.

**It is also illegal in Boston to play the fiddle**

I found [this](https://irishboston.blogspot.com/2011/04/april-1851-irish-fiddler-arrested-in.html) article which provides an image of a Boston Herald newspaper in, allegedly, April 1851. I haven't myself attempted to source this paper, however I am inclined to believe it. I wish it had more context. A transcription follows:

> COMMON FIDDLING.—An old man named Michael Lacy, who was one of the persons arrested last night, in Ann street, and who says he is unable to do anything else for a living, was before the Police Court, this morning, charged with being a common fiddler. An explanation of the avocation or calling was had, ad the court explained the law in the case, and gave an interesting history of the art of "ancient minstrelsy," and of the early history of instruments called fiddles. He also spoke of hand-organs and tambourines, and of music at musters, and other public assemblies. Those who fiddle at theatres and concerts are not amenable to the law, but those who fiddle for rogues to dance violate the statute, and are to be punished. He was ordered to pay a fine of $3 and costs.

## Peanuts in Church

The list goes on and on, rattling on about gorillas in cars, tomatos on chowder, and mules on the second floor.[^1] Some laws came right up, but many others required a good bit of digging through swaths of linkrot in order to find anything remotely resembling a signal. As I read, I kept wondering where on earth all these claims were coming from. Nevertheless, I continued diligently verifying each and every law Morgan & Morgan mentioned, until I came to the following claim:

> if you are caught eating peanuts in church, you can spend as long as one year in prison

Probably, some old law from Puritan times? I dived into Google, and then I found a book by Barbara Seuling called "You Can’t Eat Peanuts in Church and Other Little-known Laws," published in 1975. Naturally, I obtained a digital copy.

![[Peanuts in Church Laws 2.png]]

Among the first laws listed, I find the following: "To take a bath in Boston, you must have a doctor's written prescription." How familiar! As I read, more and more laws I could not substantiate appeared in the book, almost verbatim. Here is the source! I wanted to reach out to Ms. Seuling to ask if she made up any of the laws, or if she still retained her research. Unfortunately, she passed away in 2016. In the book, she wrote:

> We must assume there is, or was, a good reason for all of these laws, although many of the reasons are lost in history and others we can only guess at from existing clues.

Fitting.

Evidently, I wasn't the only one curious about such laws. I also stumbled upon an incredible 2006 article by [Michael Blanding](https://www.bostonmagazine.com/2006/05/15/my-short-happy-life-in-crime/) who, like me, spent many hours researching these laws to little avail. Unlike me, Michael went the extra step to consult with experts, who derived the same conclusion:

> The several historians and lawyers I call confirm that most of these supposed laws are urban legends — rules that seemingly everyone has heard about but no one can actually find on the books. According to the city archivist, for instance, the widely perceived bathing ordinances hearken back to a fictional 1917 story by H. L. Mencken.

So maybe my hypothesis was wrong—Ms.Seuling didn't originate these urban legends. They are a tale as old as time. I think they survive because they are super appealing. We have all, at one point or another, been made abundantly familiar with the pedanticism of our government, so these laws don't feel so far out of left field. For this reason, we are immediately predisposed to accept them as true. Furthermore, they provide room for comic relief, validating that we aren't the crazy ones. This contributes to the virality—if there's one thing we like sharing, it's a good laugh, and when we're in a laughing mood we rarely stop to ask if what we're reading is true.

With innocent laws like this, I think there's little harm in perpetuating a myth. Nobody transports a gorilla in the back of their car, and they're pretty fun. The ones with some semblance of truth often lead to real laws with real history. For example, Christmas *[was](https://www.americanheritage.com/when-christmas-was-banned-boston)* banned in Boston. Who knew?

Nevertheless, cases like this present a good proxy for examining the disinformation cycle ravaging this country. If myths proliferate this easily among the most educated population in the country, imagine how bad it must be elsewhere. Even one of the biggest *law firms* can't keep its facts straight. Accurately interpreting the law is their entire job, and they failed spectacularly. How can the general population possibly be expected to keep it together in a world of sensationalism and red herrings? Media literacy must join the core curriculum of educational institutions.
