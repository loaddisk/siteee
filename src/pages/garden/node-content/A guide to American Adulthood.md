This is a little guide to American Adulthood. I guess, first I must define adulthood. For the purposes of this guide, adulthood encompasses responsibilities you may not have had early in your life. It is entirely reasonable for some of this guide to be applicable to teenagers.

This guide is all about doing things right. It’s not a fast read by any means. Whenever possible, it explains the shortcuts people take, and the considerations that must be made beyond said shortcuts. It aims to be neutral, presenting facts whenever possible wherever there is room for an opinion.

Very rarely will this guide teach you _how_ to do something. There are plenty of guides for that. It seeks to say _what_ you should be doing. This guide is, more often than not, financial in nature. As is life.

It is important to understand who the author is. I am a male, though I have consulted with women in the production of this guide. I am a US citizen. I am not a lawyer. This is not legal or financial advice. I am not responsible for any losses you may incur.

## Basic Tasks

### Shaving

When hair begins to grow, people will notice. Nobody will tell you to shave, but they will notice. I recommend beginning early. NEVER share razor blades.

Disposable blades are a huge scam. Buy a safety razor. These razors have a high upfront cost, typically $30-60, but each blade will cost you 10 cents for the rest of your life. Within a year, you will be saving money. By the time you die, you will have saved thousands of dollars. I like the Henson. It’s a pretty expensive option, but I think you get what you pay for.

## Finance

Success in adulthood is attainable, but it requires strong financial rigor, from a young age.
### The Stock Market

The stock market, of course, is one of the more advanced topics we will cover here. The reason it is covered upfront is because it is intrinsic to the latter financial topics.

The stock market is the key to financial success. Annualized, the market has historically returned an average of 8% per year, even accounting for inflation. 8% isn’t bad, but it’s not incredible. The _real_ power is compounding. If each year, that 8% is reinvested for 40 years, you would have 22 times more money than you started with. There are many ways to trade stocks.

Buying and selling individual stocks can be profitable, but is quite risky. “Options” are contracts tied to the performance of a stock that dramatically increases the risk/reward. Frequent buying and selling is called “day trading”. Less than 1% of day traders consistently beat the market, and only 15% profit overall.

Less risky is buying and holding. But what to buy? Index funds. The S&P 500 is a slice of the 500 largest companies at any given moment, so in effect you will be owning a part of each company, making returns very consistent. This is a solid mix of risk and reward. You can also get total market indexes, which are slightly less risky.

Somewhere between are mutual funds and ETFs. In exchange for a fee, a mutual fund will invest for you according to the goals of the fund. For instance, FSPCX will focus on insurance companies. If you think insurance companies will do well, you might buy this mutual fund. Statistically, less than 50% of mutual funds will beat the market (though, not all _intend_ on beating the market). It is important to do research on mutual funds using a site like Morningstar.

I cannot stress this enough: Buy and hold! Time in market > Timing the market. Always. Also, past performance does not guarantee future results!

Where can I get an account from? Robinhood is popular, but it has very high hidden fees and encourages risky investing. I suggest getting an account at one of the larger brokerages like Fidelity, Vanguard or Schwab.
### Loans, Interest, and Debt

#### Credit Cards

Credit cards are monthly loans. The limit of these loans is based on your “credit” (a number derived from your history of paying the loans on time). If the loan is paid off on time (at the end of the month), you will not be charged interest. If you fail to pay the card off, the interest is absurdly high! If there is only *one* takeaway you get from this article, it should be "do everything in your power to avoid credit card debt". If you are anything short of 100% on top of your finances, do not get a credit card.

If you *do* get a credit card, make sure to set up autopay. Only use the credit card to buy things you could pay for out of pocket. You may ask: "Why use a credit card if I can pay out of pocket?" The first factor is fraud protection. Credit companies will be much better about protecting you from fraudulent charges than banks, because *their* money was stolen, not yours. The second reason is rewards:

If you are financially responsible, good credit scores can give you good credit cards, and good credit cards give you rewards. These rewards may be things like a 3% refund on groceries (cashback), or “points” that can be redeemed on travel. This article refuses to concern itself with suggesting cards or "credit card hacks", but you may find the [r/CreditCards](https://www.reddit.com/r/CreditCards/) sidebar to be helpful. Just know that just because you *can* doesn't mean you *should*. Temperance is key to financial independence.  
#### Banks

Money in a bank may also pay interest. It is important to keep money on hand, for emergencies and for paying off credit card bills. Remember, only spend the money you have. When considering bank accounts, look at interest rates. You’ll likely need a savings account (to earn 3-5% interest) _and_ a checking account (that you transfer money into from your savings account in order to pay credit card bills).

There are some more unorthodox strategies here as well. Some checking accounts have high yields, like Wealthfront. Wealthfront isn’t technically a bank, but they spread money between banks for FIDC insurance. Fidelity cash accounts are similar.

### Retirement, Account Types, Planning

When you start out at a brokerage like Fidelity, you will likely have a _brokerage account_. This is one of the many account types offered. To understand the different types of accounts, first we will need to understand taxes.
#### A primer on taxes

Income is something that adds to your personal capital. Typical sources of income include paychecks, dividends, gifts, and interest. Your income is taxed. 

When you receive a paycheck, some of the money should be set aside to pay the tax man. The amount the IRS will want depends on your income, and these calculations will be described in a latter section. For now, all we need to know is that the IRS will want _something_, and that there are ways to reduce the amount they want.

So when you receive the paycheck, the IRS will want some. The rest is yours to do as you please with. Let’s say you invest it. You put it in the market, and in return you receive a number of shares. Hopefully, over time, the value of the shares you hold increases. But shares are not currency. Eventually, you sell your shares to buy something. In return you receive money. This money is income, and the IRS wants some. This is the _capital gains tax_.

Tax sucks. Luckily, the feds offer us a number of accounts with certain restrictions that can reduce our tax burden.
#### Individual Retirement Accounts

The _IRA_, or the _Individual Retirement Account,_ is a tax advantaged account any individual can open. As it says on the tin, the goal of the IRA is to save for retirement, and the restrictions are pursuant to this goal. The typical rule is that you cannot withdrawal from these accounts until you turn 59.5, but there are plenty of other rules and exceptions. The maximum yearly contribution is 7k, and occasionally increases with inflation. There are two types of IRAs: _Traditional_ and _Roth_.

Traditional IRAs are _tax deferred_. The contributions you make to the IRA are subtracted off the income you report that year, therefore decreasing your tax burden. The _withdrawals_ you will eventually make will be taxed.

Roth IRAs, by contrast, are taxed upfront. You must make less than $161k a year to contribute. The amount you eventually _withdraw_ will be tax exempt. Provided you can use a Roth IRA, the choice between Roth and Traditional should be based on this question: Will I be *taxed* more in retirement than I am right now? I cannot possibly state this enough: This question is *not* "will I *make* more now or in retirement". It is a tax question. We will elaborate on this question later.

With Roth IRAs, you can withdrawal the principal (the amount you contributed directly) at any time without taxation. The IRS allows this because you've already paid tax. This is not advisable, because you'd be reducing the amount in the tax-free-growth pool.

With both IRAs and Roth IRAs, there are cases where the early withdrawal penalty can be avoided. Research these cases, but don't plan for them. The one time it may make sense is a Roth Conversion Ladder & SEPP, which allows for early retirement. This can safely be filed away as a "later problem".
#### 401(k)s

If your employer offers a 401(k), this is a powerful retirement vehicle. 401ks (and Roth 401ks) are mostly defined by some employer contribution matching, and a higher contribution limit (currently 23k). 401(k)s can be used alongside IRAs. 
#### Less tax in retirement + Traditional or Roth?

It is a common misunderstanding that the proportional tax rate will be the same between those who are employed, and those who are retired. In reality, those with a good mix of accounts will be paying less tax in retirement. Tax on investment earnings is called "capital gains". The capital gains tax is lower.

- Seniors have a standard taxable income deduction, which is currently 14k if single and 28k if married.
- In taxable accounts (a brokerage), the tax rate on capital gains is 0% up to 45k in income, 15% up to 500k, and 20% beyond that. If married and filing jointly, the tiers are roughly doubled. In retirement, you have less income.
- Traditional 401k withdrawals up to $23k are at 10%, and up to $94k are at 12%.
- Roth withdrawals are not taxed.

Imagine withdrawing 100k in a year from a combination of these sources. Will you be paying more or less than you would be now? Here's another one: If you contribute *more* to a traditional than a Roth due to the extra money from tax deferral, does that put you in a better spot?

There's much more to this subject. If you're really young, Roth may very likely be the way to go.

#### HSAs

Some takeaways from above:

1. Tax advantaged accounts are good.
2. It is confusing to decide between Roth, Traditional, or both

Here's what isn't confusing. HSAs are hands down the best retirement vehicle. To qualify, you must be

1. On a HDHP (High-Deductible Health Plan)
2. Not be enrolled in Medicare
3. Not claimed as a dependent on someone else's tax return

The decision between a HDHP and a normal health plan, like many things, is complicated. We will discuss insurance below. If a HDHP *is* right for you, read on.

HSAs have a yearly contribution limit, $4,300 in 2024. They are *triple tax advantaged*, meaning you can deduct your contribution, grow the account tax-free, and withdrawal tax free. You can use the funds at any time for a qualified medical expense, and withdrawal for any reason at the age of 65 or later.
#### Stupid tax hacks

If you began investing at age 18 with a principle of $1000, and contributed $300 dollars a month into a fund with 10% APY, you would have gained something around $3800 by the time you're 22. Assuming these funds were not in a tax sheltered account, and you withdraw when you're making above $60k a year, the capital gains (that \$3800) is subject to a 15% capital gains tax (\$570). You, however, are smart. While you are still a low income college student, you sell all your shares and immediately purchase them for the same price. In doing so, you have incurred long term capital gains, but these gains were taxed at 0% due to your low income. When you buy, your basis is reset, eliminating this potential capital gains exposure. Please note that you can only do this with ETFs and stocks. Don't try it with mutual funds. These are priced at the end of the day, for one, so you'd be exposing yourself to a days worth of market movement. Second, most mutual funds have strict limits on rapid buy and sell orders. In theory, I'd figure you'd be best off buying VOO with its .03% annual fee for four years, selling VOO at 3:30PM or so, and then creating a buy order for the FXAIX mutual fund with its .015% fee. #### Cars

There will be exceptions to the advice below. It is not law.

First, know that cars are depreciating assets. In other words, they are a liability. There are three mechanisms for acquiring a car. A lease, a loan, and outright purchasing it.

A lease only saves you money if you change cars every 3-4 years. You may very well save money on maintenance and such, tying into the piece of mind home factor, but overall you will lose money. And maybe you're ok with that.

A *loan* is an incentive for you to buy a car you cannot afford. Only buy cars that you *could* pay outright for. If you know how to leverage loans correctly, however, they could save you money. If the market returns an average of 8% a year, and your loan is at 2.5% APR, you would be saving thousands of dollars by keeping the money in the market. But only take the loan if you would've got the car without said loan.
