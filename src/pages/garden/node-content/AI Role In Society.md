## Startup culture

A couple of times, when I mentioned that I don't think my product is anywhere close to ready, I've been told that I should ship an MVP fast, without worrying about a perfect launch:

- [Don't STRESS launch, soft launch!](https://stackingthebricks.com/dont-stress-launch-soft-launch/)
- [A Minimum Viable Product Is Not a Product, It's a Process](https://www.ycombinator.com/blog/minimum-viable-product-process/)
- [Lessons Learned: Don't launch](https://www.startuplessonslearned.com/2009/03/dont-launch.html)
- [How We Fooled Ourselves into Delaying Our Startup’s Launch](http://viniciusvacanti.com/2012/04/02/how-we-fooled-ourselves-into-delaying-our-startups-launch/)

In my mind, the reason for this advice basically boils down to fear. Startup founders are shockingly risk-adverse, and I think this aversion leads to problems in the long run. I imagine most venture capital funded startups are run by people that already have significant capital. These people wish to *avoid* using this capital as runway, in the chance their product fails. In addition, they want to spend less time building something that can fail (so they hire programmers really early on, diminishing the output of each individual programmer with each hire). And sure, that makes sense! The common advice is that 90% of startups fail. But *why* do they fail? I think this failure is *caused* by trying to grow too fast.

![[Pasted image 20240604092723.png]]

Specifically

1. It is well understood that venture capital requires you to either go public, fail, or sell to a larger company
2. Two of these routes creates incentives for quick, consistent ROI
3. A common belief, currently, is that the drive to [grow forever](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/the-delusion-of-infinite-economic-growth/) is [what makes businesses "enshittify"](https://pluralistic.net/2023/01/21/potemkin-ai/#hey-guys)
4. But if the business does not seek external capital, there is no need for growth beyond the desire of the individual[^1] 

The con of this is that, obviously, growth will be immensely slow. And particularly risky for the founder when contrasted with venture backed models. But it will be good for the consumer. And people *love* labours of love.

I'm about to go on the first of many whims here: Anybody who claims they are creating a business out of altruistic motif *while* giving up equity must, to a certain extent, be lying:

And here, I think we're just chasing fads. But that makes sense. If you have received 500 thousand dollars from a venture capitalist, the venture capitalist will not accept you telling them that you missed the wave and intend to row to shore. They want you on the next wave, outbound in an utterly different direction. Plenty of us can row, but very few catch the surf.

Can AI amplify knowledge?

AI note-taking is like a kid reading sparknotes. They go through all the motions, they put on this facade of education, but they’re not genuinely interested. They just want to be perceived this way, as it is advantageous in society. And to be perfectly clear, there’s often merit to this approach of “playing the game”. Everyone must if they wish to survive. But it’s undeniable that simply playing won't contribute to the purported goal of furthering knowledge. To do that, you must love the game for what it is.

> Have you used ChatGPT 4 or Gemini Ultra extensively? I am surprised that this is your conclusion

 In terms of actual conversation, I’ve found hallucinations particularly frustrating. The domain that LLMs excel at is, seemingly, doing things that you already know how to do. They will get 80% of the way, and they will make mistakes while doing so. And then you spend 30% of the time correcting them. But that's worth it, because you saved 40%.

But this isn't producing knowledge. You must already *know* to make effective use of these tools. And AI *certainly* cannot create new knowledge. Which brings us to the other problem, the even more disastrous one. If we decide that AI is better at informing us than humans, society would stagnate. Nobody would go out and produce knowledge (ie: journalists) if the pipeline was selling the knowledge to the AI company, and the AI regurgitating it while missing the touch of human nuance. As I've stated before, LLMs are tuned to produce the most average opinion, by the nature of their corpus. But that is the world we are rapidly approaching.

AI centralizes power and knowledge in the hands of the few companies who can afford to burn money for GPU, which I think is completely contrary to our ideals of empowering individuals. And why do they burn money for GPU? That’s not sustainable! Because they want to win. It’s the VC zero-sum game all over again. It’s literally just burning money until all the other parties tap out. The world’s richest companies are in competitions to see who runs out of money first. Startup founders think they're capitalizing on some sort of new wave, but this is what the richest companies want. It only benefits them in the end, because this is how you blitzscale. First you're good, and then you enshittify.

## Can VC startups be good? Thoughts on Daylight

![[Pasted image 20240604110933.png]]

daylight computer is a new device that I am excited about. They have a very similar goal to me — the founder talks of that long forgotten time when computers worked for people. He's worked on it for 6 years, quietly. When he came on a startup promo channel, he said how scared he was to show it to the world. Nice! Slow growth! Making something really, really great, a labor of love. The company was incorporated as a PBC. But they took investment.

The legal responsibility of a normal, public business is to grow forever, but I'm sure we are all aware of some (usually private) businesses that remain small, scoped, and dedicated to their craft. PBCs tell shareholders that their responsibility is to grow for as long as growth increases their ability to fulfill the stated mission. I've painted venture capital in a pretty poor light. Sometimes, however, the embarking is too great to undertake alone.

Furthermore, companies founded with the goal of creating profit by being good to consumers are in a much better place. Take Apple. I know that Apple has done some incredibly questionable things. Still, if they started making iPhones in plastic, the company would fall apart. Well-built hardware *is* their thing. They became one of the richest companies in the world through it. So if Daylight is launching by saying "we will be profitable by being good to consumers", that is a good place to be starting I think, as opposed to the conventional "we'll figure it out later". In rare cases, I think companies can become wealthy by making good products.
## In conclusion, the startup bible

Consumers love labors of love, because the relationship just feels right. Instead of something rushed that's bought with resentment, like an EA game, Individual founders might do better to poise themselves as the Team Cherry of software. People buy their games with confidence, knowing it will be worth their money and time, and with confidence as they know that somebody poured their heart into the product they're paying for.

Taking investment does not necessarily mean your product isn't a labour of love. But it becomes a much harder sell, I think. Founders aren't stupid, they know what cycle they're getting into. Still, sometimes immense capital is required to get off the ground, so in some cases it might be an unfortunate means to an end. In these cases, though, it's actually *more* of a risk. Not only are you risking failure, you're risking losing control of something you care for. Also, it is naturally hard for outsiders to judge your motivation.

Yes, it's risky. I'm not saying the risk is right for everyone. Plenty of "labor of love" games go unnoticed and fade into obscurity. Funding and failing fast alleviates that risk. But if you take that risk, and if you win, I think you might win life.
