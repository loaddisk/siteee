> If a thief could only take from you what you failed to notice, how much would you be willing to lose?

Contrary to popular belief, life is not inherently short. It only appears so when it is not fully lived. When our days dissolve into distraction and mindless divulgence, time seems to vanish before our very eyes.

This loss is rarely dramatic. It does not come from missing a singular, defining moment, but from something quieter and far more insidious: the steady erosion of our attention. Time is taken from us in small sentences, too small to notice, yet large enough to empty entire years of substance.

And more often than not, we surrender it willingly.

- To trivialities  - To idle consumption  - To ceaseless distraction 

We call this “‘rest”, “‘relaxation” even, but true rest restores, while this - bears no trace. Nothing is gained nor is anything meaningful retained. Such time is not merely spent but erased. For what is not attended to is not truly lived.

The difference, then, is not found in the clock itself, but in our awareness of its presence. Time does not expand through speed or efficiency, but through attendance. A single hour, fully inhabited, carries more weight than a day passed in the abstract. Life stretches in proportion to: what we notice, our engagement and what we allow to impress itself upon us.

And so, when we pause, when we deliberately interrupt the automatic process, we regain authorship over our actions. We can ask: Is this worthy of my time?

This is not a call to urgency, nor to the anxious streamlining of every single moment either, to rush this process would be to undermine it. A life dissected into constant self-correction becomes just another form of distraction. Depth cannot be forced and attention simply cannot be sustained through strain alone.

Life expands when it has texture and texture is not a product of speed, but of depth.

It is found in sustained focus and meaningful effort. It is built through moments that are fully registered rather than half-experienced. Even “rest”, when it is intentional, contributes to this texture - rest that restores rather than numbs, that allows the mind to settle rather than scatter.

The goal, then, is not to fill time, but to inhabit it.

In doing so, life does not become longer in years but it becomes longer in experience. And that, perhaps, is the only measure that has ever truly mattered, to me at least.
