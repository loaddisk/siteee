TikTok will be banned in 18 days. I don't like TikTok. My communities aren't on it. It doesn't make me laugh. It's always trying to sell me some cheap junk. Every time I open it, I get this small, tingling feeling that somebody's watching me, to an even greater degree than I get from Facebook's products. And, if I'm going to be honest, I'm more of a Reels guy anyway. With all this said, you might be surprised to learn that I am opposed to the platform's impending demise by the hand of the United States Supreme Court.

I mean, first, I fail to see how it poses a national security threat. I do not contest that the app is controlled by a so-called "adversary". I do not contest that the app poses [a threat](https://krausefx.com/blog/announcing-inappbrowsercom-see-what-javascript-commands-get-executed-in-an-in-app-browser) to *individual security*. But this is a free country, and I should be free to broadcast [every waking moment](https://www.bbc.com/news/magazine-37681006) of my life to the whole wide world if I choose to do so. It's not like consumers *aren't* aware of the privacy implications of the app — this long, drawn-out battle has made Uncle Sam's concerns quite apparent. Consumers have chosen that their privacy is an acceptable cost for the platform's convenience.

Of course, maybe the government *should* step in. Sometimes, consumers are backed against a wall, finding their communities entrenched in platforms that continue to enshittify. The government's job *should* be to regulate impartially, so that the relationship between individuals and corporations is an exchange on equal terms. The problem is, this regulation is targeted. It doesn't apply to America's darling tech companies, which do much of the same things completely scot-free. We've been asking for solid privacy regulation for *years*, and our voices go unheard. I don't feel good about this targeted attack against one company whilst my broader concerns go ignored.

[If the TikTok bill is about privacy, why aren’t lawmakers passing comprehensive privacy laws?](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/03/5-big-unanswered-questions-about-tiktok-bill)

But why TikTok? Well, it's Chinese. And China is the enemy. And only *American* tech companies should be allowed to uncontrollably harvest their user's data. Written out like that, this isolationist defense is laughable at best. So instead, it's a threat to national security. But how can this be? It would be one thing if Bytedance was building out our telecommunications infrastructure. Sure, it *should* be encrypted, but it isn't, *and* there is an expectation of privacy. I imagine it's quite tempting, and rewarding, to snoop on telecommunications. I can concede that it would be bad if China wiretapped us.

![[Pasted image 20250101201902.png]]

But Bytedance *isn't* building out telecommunications infrastructure. It makes an app for teenagers to broadcast funny dances to the entire world. By the platform's very nature, there is no expectation of privacy. So what, exactly, does the United States believe is leaking to China? Metadata? It [sure would be bad](https://www.cs.umd.edu/~dml/papers/wifi-surveillance-sp24.pdf) if a foreign nation could map out the location of our troops in real-time. So... ban it on the devices of those [employed by the government](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2023/02/M-23-13-No-TikTok-on-Government-Devices-Implementation-Guidance_final.pdf)? Maybe, our congressmen[^2] believe that the platform installs some sort of malware. On mobile phones? The vulnerabilities [that would enable this](https://googleprojectzero.blogspot.com/2021/12/a-deep-dive-into-nso-zero-click.html) go for millions of dollars and are typically deployed to a small group of journalists before inevitably being detected and burned. *Not* on the scale of 1 billion users.

Maybe, the government fears that China is misusing the platform to spread a political narrative. But, it's ok, encouraged even, for [Russia](https://www.nbcnews.com/think/opinion/senate-russia-report-proves-trump-was-wrong-mueller-was-right-ncna1237743) or a [narcissistic man child](https://apnews.com/article/elon-musk-america-pac-1-million-giveaway-donald-trump-5d918a79d4c35c9d1f9a46f1766bf6ff) to butt their heads in. Talk about double standards.[^1] The evidence of interference by TikTok, as far as I can tell, is anecdotal at best. [Under oath](https://www.techpolicy.press/transcript-tiktok-ceo-testifies-to-congress/), Shou Chew stated "We do not promote or remove content at the request of the Chinese government." I wouldn't say I'm inclined to believe the words of a big tech leader, and I'd like to acknowledge that despite best intentions, things can go awry, and *yet*, what evidence do we have that the platform was systematically weaponized against American democracy? Is that evidence stronger than the evidence of similar behavior from Twitter under Elon Musk?

The government's failure to act on its concerns in any sort of timely manner has introduced a new problem. TikTok grew. As much as it pains me to say this, it has become a bastion for the free transfer of ideas among America's youth — 1/3rd report getting their news from the app. And, it just so happens that this freedom of expression has led to a [drift](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2023/dec/11/tiktok-social-media-young-people-moral-panic) away from the government's own narrative, a sort of radicalization that could [anger lawmakers](https://www.inquirer.com/columnists/attytood/tiktok-ban-protests-gaza-israel-houston-flooding-climate-change-20240507.html). If it is true that [it’s not the algorithm, teens are just pro-palestine](https://www.vice.com/en/article/tiktok-its-not-the-algorithm-teens-are-just-pro-palestine/), then this ban is a direct attack on the youth's freedom of speech, 63% of which use TikTok daily. The cat has left the bag.

[If the TikTok bill is about content, how will it avoid violating the First Amendment? ](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/03/5-big-unanswered-questions-about-tiktok-bill)

Like I said, I don't like TikTok. I try to avoid it as much as possible. I wish it didn't happen.  But it happened. It's here. Also, it's hardly special. It's like literally every other big tech company. I would kill for some solid, sweeping privacy regulation. But we don't have it, and until we do, we mustn't gaslight ourselves into complacency with the nonsensical, hypocritical arguments used as justification for targeted attacks on our freedom.

[^1]: I want to note that the evidence of *successful* Russian interference isn't [particularly](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-022-35576-9) strong. My gut feeling is that it's worse than these numbers would suggest (they keep doing it, after all), but I'm a big fan of numbers and I want to be clear when my opinion isn't strongly grounded in fact.
[^2]: Our congressmen [do not](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t-lMIGV-dUI) understand technology. Their beliefs about it cannot be given much credibility.
